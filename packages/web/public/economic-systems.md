# Major Economic Systems — Rules, Definitions & Success Parameters

Reference for **The Benevolent Monarch**: how the world's major economic systems are defined, what rules govern them, and what measurable outcomes indicate success or failure.

> **Note on spelling:** *Tripartism* (also written *triparism*) refers to structured cooperation among three social partners — government, labor, and employers.

---

## How to Read This Document

Each system is described with:

| Section | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| **Definition** | What the system is and what problem it tries to solve |
| **Core Rules** | Non-negotiable operating principles |
| **Key Institutions** | Organizations and mechanisms that enforce the rules |
| **Success Parameters** | Quantifiable indicators that the system is working |
| **Failure Signals** | Warning signs that the system is breaking down |
| **Examples** | Historical or contemporary reference points |

Success parameters are framed so they can map to simulation metrics (GDP growth, inequality index, unemployment, stability, etc.).

## Shared Vocabulary

| Term | Definition |
| --- | --- |
| **Means of production** | Land, factories, infrastructure, capital equipment used to create goods and services |
| **Private property** | Assets owned and controlled by individuals or firms, with legal exclusion of others |
| **Public ownership** | Assets controlled by the state or collectively by citizens |
| **Price mechanism** | Supply and demand setting prices through voluntary exchange |
| **Central planning** | Government or party authorities allocating resources by directive rather than market prices |
| **Redistribution** | Transfer of income or wealth from one group to another via taxes, subsidies, or services |
| **Social partners** | In tripartism: government, employer organizations, and trade unions negotiating policy jointly |

## 1. Capitalism

### Definition

An economic system in which **private individuals and firms own the means of production**, allocate resources through **markets and prices**, and pursue **profit** as the primary incentive. The state's role is typically limited to enforcing contracts, property rights, and competition — though real-world capitalism always includes some regulation.

### Core Rules

1. **Private ownership** of capital, land, and enterprise is legally protected.
2. **Voluntary exchange** — buyers and sellers transact by mutual consent.
3. **Price discovery** — prices reflect supply, demand, and competition rather than central decree.
4. **Profit motive** — firms seek surplus; losses discipline inefficient actors.
5. **Wage labor** — most people sell labor for income rather than owning their full output.
6. **Limited liability & contract law** — legal frameworks enable investment and trade at scale.

### Key Institutions

- Stock markets and banks
- Central banks (monetary policy)
- Courts enforcing property and contract
- Competition regulators (in mature systems)
- Patent and intellectual property systems

### Success Parameters

| Parameter | Target Range | Why It Matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Real GDP growth | 2–4% annually (developed); higher in emerging economies | Indicates productive capacity expanding |
| Unemployment | < 5% (frictional + structural) | Labor markets clearing efficiently |
| Inflation | 2–3% annually (stable expectations) | Predictable money value |
| Business formation rate | Net positive new firms/year | Innovation and competition alive |
| Gini coefficient | Context-dependent; extreme > 0.50 is a stress signal | Wealth concentration manageable |
| Productivity growth | Positive per-worker output trend | Capital and technology deployed well |
| Rule of law index | High and stable | Property rights enforceable |

### Failure Signals

- Monopolies or oligopolies suppress competition
- Boom-bust cycles without recovery (depressions)
- Mass unemployment with idle capacity
- Environmental or social costs externalized without correction
- Financial crises from unregulated speculation

### Examples

United States (market-oriented), Singapore, Hong Kong, post-1980s Chile

### Variants

- **Laissez-faire capitalism** — minimal state intervention
- **Welfare capitalism** — markets plus social safety nets (see Mixed Economy)

## 2. Socialism

### Definition

An economic system oriented toward **social ownership** or **strong democratic control** of the means of production, with the goal of **reducing class inequality** and meeting human needs rather than maximizing private profit. Socialism spans a wide spectrum — from democratic market socialism to near-total state ownership.

### Core Rules

1. **Social ownership** — key industries, utilities, or the entire economy owned by the state, cooperatives, or worker collectives.
2. **Planned or guided allocation** — government sets priorities for housing, healthcare, education, infrastructure.
3. **Redistribution** — progressive taxation, universal services, wage floors, or profit-sharing.
4. **Worker rights** — collective bargaining, workplace democracy, or union power elevated.
5. **De-emphasis on pure profit** — firms may operate to serve social goals, not only shareholders.

### Key Institutions

- National health and education systems
- Public utilities and transport
- Sovereign wealth or social funds
- Strong trade unions
- Central planning ministries (in more centralized forms)

### Success Parameters

| Parameter | Target Range | Why It Matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Poverty rate | Declining; near-zero extreme poverty | Core equity goal |
| Access to essentials | > 95% coverage for healthcare, education, housing | Needs met regardless of income |
| Wage share of GDP | Stable or rising labor share | Workers benefit from output |
| Gini coefficient | Lower than comparable capitalist peers | Inequality reduced |
| Social mobility index | Improving intergenerational mobility | Class boundaries weakening |
| Public service quality | High satisfaction / low wait times | State delivery works |
| Fiscal sustainability | Deficits manageable without hyperinflation | System economically viable |

### Failure Signals

- Bureaucratic stagnation — queues, shortages, low innovation
- Brain drain — skilled workers emigrate
- Fiscal crisis — unsustainable deficits or debt
- Political capture — elites control "social" property for private gain
- Suppressed productivity — no incentive to improve efficiency

### Examples

Nordic social democracies (Sweden, Norway — mixed), Cuba (state-led), cooperative economies (Mondragón, Spain)

### Variants

- **Democratic socialism** — socialism via electoral democracy
- **Market socialism** — social ownership with market pricing (see Section 8)

## 3. Tripartism

*Also spelled triparism.*

### Definition

A **neo-corporatist** system built on **structured cooperation among three social partners**: the **government**, **employer organizations**, and **trade unions**. Rather than leaving wages and working conditions purely to market conflict or state decree, the three parties negotiate binding or semi-binding agreements on economic and labor policy.

Tripartism typically operates **within a mixed economy** — markets still exist, but macro-level outcomes (wages, hours, benefits, training) are co-determined.

### Core Rules

1. **Tripartite negotiation** — government, unions, and employers meet as equals (or near-equals) at national or sectoral level.
2. **Consensus over confrontation** — strikes and lockouts are last resorts; bargaining is institutionalized.
3. **Macro coordination** — wage policy aligned with inflation, productivity, and employment targets.
4. **Binding or semi-binding agreements** — national income policies, sectoral contracts, or social pacts.
5. **Government as broker and enforcer** — state facilitates deals and may legislate agreed terms.
6. **Mixed economy base** — private enterprise remains, but labor relations are socially regulated.

### Key Institutions

- National tripartite councils or economic councils
- Peak employer federations and national union confederations
- Wage boards or income policy commissions
- Sectoral bargaining frameworks
- Works councils (firm-level co-determination)

### Success Parameters

| Parameter | Target Range | Why It Matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Strike days lost | Low relative to GDP (e.g., < 50 days/1,000 workers/year) | Industrial peace via negotiation |
| Wage–productivity alignment | Wages track productivity without runaway inflation | Macro stability |
| Unemployment | Low and stable | Partners coordinate employment policy |
| Union density | Moderate to high (often 50–80% in peak systems) | Labor has real bargaining power |
| Social pact renewal rate | Regular successful renegotiation | Institutions remain legitimate |
| Inequality (Gini) | Moderate — lower than pure market systems | Gains shared across partners |
| Trust index (inter-partner) | High mutual trust among government, unions, firms | Cooperation sustainable |

### Failure Signals

- Partner withdrawal — government, unions, or employers stop participating
- Wage-price spirals despite tripartite structure
- Corporatist capture — insiders protect incumbents, block outsiders
- Union busting or employer flight — one partner undermined
- Stagflation — coordination fails to control inflation and unemployment simultaneously

### Examples

Austria, Netherlands (polder model), Finland (national income policy agreements), Germany (co-determination), Ireland (social partnership), post-war Western Europe

### Relation to Other Systems

Tripartism is **not a full alternative** to capitalism or socialism — it is a **governance model for labor and macro policy**, usually layered on a **mixed economy** or **social market economy**.

## 4. Communism (Marxist-Leninist / Command)

### Definition

A system in which the **means of production are collectively owned** and the **state (or party) directs economic activity** through central planning, aiming for a **classless society** without private profit. In practice, historical communist states have been **one-party command economies** with near-total state ownership.

### Core Rules

1. **State ownership** of industry, land, and major assets.
2. **Central planning** — production quotas, allocation targets, and five-year plans.
3. **No private capital accumulation** — profit-seeking firms eliminated or nationalized.
4. **Party-state authority** — economic and political power unified.
5. **Job guarantee (theoretical)** — employment provided by the state.
6. **Fixed or administered prices** — prices set by planners, not markets.

### Key Institutions

- Central planning commission / Gosplan-style bodies
- State-owned enterprises (SOEs)
- Single ruling party
- State distribution and rationing networks
- Secret police or ideological enforcement (historically)

### Success Parameters

| Parameter | Target Range | Why It Matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Industrial output growth | High in early industrialization phases | Rapid catch-up industrialization |
| Literacy & basic needs | Near-universal education and healthcare | Human development goals |
| Employment rate | Near-full formal employment | Job guarantee honored |
| Heavy industry capacity | Steel, energy, infrastructure targets met | Planned priorities achieved |
| Inequality (official) | Low measured income spread | Class abolition in theory |

### Failure Signals

- Chronic shortages of consumer goods
- Low total factor productivity vs. market economies
- Innovation stagnation — no competitive pressure
- Black markets and corruption undermine planning
- Agricultural crises (e.g., collectivization failures)
- Mass political repression tied to economic control

### Examples

Soviet Union (1922–1991), Maoist China (pre-reform), North Korea, Cuba (partial)

> **Distinction:** *Communism* as an ideology envisions a stateless, moneyless end state. *Command economies* describe how communist states have actually operated.

## 5. Mixed Economy

### Definition

An economic system combining **market allocation** with **significant government intervention** — regulation, public ownership of key sectors, redistribution, and macroeconomic stabilization. Most modern developed nations are mixed economies.

### Core Rules

1. **Private markets** operate for most goods and services.
2. **Public provision** for goods with positive externalities (education, defense, infrastructure).
3. **Regulation** corrects market failures (pollution, monopoly, financial risk).
4. **Redistributive taxation** funds welfare and public goods.
5. **Counter-cyclical policy** — fiscal and monetary tools stabilize recessions.
6. **Public–private boundaries** negotiated politically, not fixed.

### Key Institutions

- Welfare states (pensions, unemployment insurance)
- Independent central banks
- Regulatory agencies (EPA, FDA, financial regulators)
- Public healthcare and education
- Progressive tax systems

### Success Parameters

| Parameter | Target Range | Why It Matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| GDP per capita growth | Steady positive trend | Prosperity maintained |
| Human Development Index (HDI) | > 0.80 (developed) | Health, education, income balanced |
| Public debt / GDP | Sustainable (< 90–120% depending on context) | Fiscal room for intervention |
| Market freedom + social spending | Both indices reasonably high | Balance achieved |
| Child poverty rate | Low and falling | Redistribution effective |
| Infrastructure quality index | High | Public investment working |

### Failure Signals

- Regulatory capture — rules serve incumbents
- Welfare state unsustainability — debt spiral
- Market fundamentalism erodes public goods
- Stagflation or prolonged austerity
- Polarization prevents policy adjustment

### Examples

Germany (social market economy), France, Canada, Japan, most EU member states

## 6. Mercantilism

### Definition

A **pre-industrial and early modern** system treating the economy as a **zero-sum competition among nations**, where wealth is measured in **accumulated bullion (gold/silver)** and success means **exporting more than importing**. State power and commerce are fused to build national advantage.

### Core Rules

1. **Trade surplus** — maximize exports, minimize imports.
2. **State-granted monopolies** — chartered companies (e.g., East India Company).
3. **Tariffs and quotas** — protect domestic industry from foreign competition.
4. **Colonial extraction** — colonies supply raw materials; metropole supplies finished goods.
5. **Bullion accumulation** — precious metals as national wealth reserve.
6. **Population and labor** as state resources for production and military power.

### Key Institutions

- Royal charters and trading monopolies
- Customs houses and tariff schedules
- Colonial administrations
- National treasuries hoarding specie
- Navigation acts restricting shipping

### Success Parameters

| Parameter | Target Range | Why It Matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Trade balance | Persistent surplus | Bullion inflow |
| Colonial revenue | Rising tribute and resource flow | Empire subsidizes metropole |
| Domestic manufacturing share | Growing vs. imports | Import substitution working |
| Treasury reserves | Rising gold/silver stock | National wealth metric |
| Naval / military capacity | Dominant relative to rivals | Power protects trade routes |

### Failure Signals

- Rival nations adopt same strategy — trade wars
- Colonial revolt disrupts supply chains
- Bullion drain despite surpluses (price inflation from metal inflow)
- Domestic consumers pay high prices due to protectionism
- Innovation lags — monopolies face no pressure to improve

### Examples

17th–18th century Britain, France, Spain, Portugal; influenced early American trade policy

> **Historical note:** Mercantilism is largely obsolete as a governing ideology but **neo-mercantilist** trade practices (strategic tariffs, export subsidies) persist.

## 7. Feudalism

### Definition

A **land-based hierarchical system** in which **lords grant land (fiefs) to vassals** in exchange for **military service and loyalty**, and **peasants (serfs) work the land** in return for protection. Economic life is **localized, agrarian, and obligation-based** rather than market-based.

### Core Rules

1. **Land as the primary form of wealth** — not liquid capital.
2. **Hierarchical obligations** — vassalage chains from king to knight to peasant.
3. **Serfdom** — peasants bound to land; cannot freely leave or sell labor.
4. **Barter and subsistence** — limited long-distance trade; local production for local consumption.
5. **Military service** in exchange for tenure and protection.
6. **Custom and tradition** govern obligations more than written contract law.

### Key Institutions

- Manorial estates
- Feudal courts and lordly justice
- Guilds (in later feudal/early market transition)
- Church landholdings and tithes
- Hereditary titles and succession

### Success Parameters

| Parameter | Target Range | Why It Matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Agricultural surplus | Enough to feed population + lord's retinue | System sustains hierarchy |
| Military loyalty | Vassals honor feudal contracts | Political order maintained |
| Population stability | Slow growth; low famine frequency | Agrarian base stable |
| Local trade volume | Modest but functioning fairs and markets | Some exchange beyond subsistence |
| Church-state balance | Cooperative power sharing | Legitimacy of hierarchy |

### Failure Signals

- Famine and plague collapse labor base
- Vassal revolt — obligations ignored
- Commercial revolution bypasses feudal lords
- Enclosure or monetization breaks serf bonds
- Central monarchy weakens local lords beyond repair

### Examples

Medieval Western Europe (9th–15th centuries), Tokugawa Japan (bakuhan system, analogous features)

## 8. Market Socialism

### Definition

A hybrid attempting to combine **social ownership of enterprises** with **market pricing and competition**. Workers or the public own firms, but those firms compete, set prices, and respond to demand — unlike pure command planning.

### Core Rules

1. **Social ownership** — firms owned by workers, municipalities, or public investment funds.
2. **Market prices** — goods and services priced by supply and demand.
3. **Democratic firm governance** — workers vote on management and profit distribution.
4. **Profit sharing** — surplus distributed among workers or reinvested socially.
5. **State sets framework** — macro policy, regulation, and ownership rules, not micro quotas.

### Success Parameters

| Parameter | Target Range | Why It Matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Cooperative firm survival rate | High multi-decade survival | Model is durable |
| Worker income vs. capitalist peers | Equal or higher | Ownership delivers gains |
| Productivity | Comparable to private competitors | Markets discipline efficiency |
| Inequality within firms | Low wage spread | Democratic ethos upheld |
| Reinvestment rate | Adequate R&D and capital renewal | Not just short-term distribution |

### Failure Signals

- Cooperatives under-invest due to profit distribution pressure
- State interference overrides worker democracy
- Unable to raise capital at scale
- Outcompeted by private firms with more aggressive reinvestment

### Examples

Mondragón Corporation (Basque Country, Spain), Yugoslav self-management (historical), modern proposals in academic literature

## 9. State Capitalism

### Definition

A system where the **state acts as the primary economic actor**, owning or directing major industries while using **market tools** (profit, exports, stock listings) to compete globally. Political authority and commercial power are closely linked.

### Core Rules

1. **SOEs dominate** strategic sectors — energy, finance, telecom, transport.
2. **State-guided investment** — industrial policy picks winners.
3. **Markets used instrumentally** — for exports, pricing some goods, attracting capital.
4. **Political loyalty** may influence enterprise leadership.
5. **Sovereign wealth** accumulates from state enterprise profits.

### Success Parameters

| Parameter | Target Range | Why It Matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| SOE return on assets | Positive and improving | State firms not pure drains |
| Infrastructure buildout | Rapid (rail, ports, power) | Industrial policy delivers |
| Export market share | Rising in targeted sectors | Global competitiveness |
| GDP growth | Sustained high growth phases | Catch-up industrialization |
| Sovereign wealth fund size | Growing reserves | Fiscal buffer created |

### Failure Signals

- SOE inefficiency — political appointments over competence
- Overcapacity from directed lending
- Corruption and opaque accounting
- Private sector crowded out
- Debt-fueled growth without productivity gains

### Examples

China (post-reform SOE model), Singapore (Temasek/GIC), UAE, Russia (energy-dominated)

## 10. Anarcho-Capitalism

### Definition

A theoretical system with **no state**, where **all property is private**, and **law, security, and dispute resolution** are provided by competing private agencies. All economic coordination is voluntary and contractual.

### Core Rules

1. **No taxation or government** — all services privately purchased.
2. **Absolute private property** — including roads, courts, and defense.
3. **Voluntary contracts only** — no coerced redistribution.
4. **Competing legal systems** — private arbitration replaces state courts.
5. **Free banking** — no central bank; currency competition.

### Success Parameters (Theoretical)

| Parameter | Target Range | Why It Matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Contract enforcement rate | Near 100% via private courts | Order without state |
| Voluntary exchange volume | High and growing | Markets fully free |
| Security cost as % of income | Low — competition drives efficiency | Private defense affordable |
| Innovation rate | Very high — no regulatory barriers | Pure market dynamism |

### Failure Signals (Observed in Critique)

- Monopoly private armies
- Justice skewed toward wealthiest buyers
- Public goods (pollution control, epidemics) unaddressed
- No mechanism for poverty relief
- Power vacuum filled by warlords

### Examples

No full-scale real-world implementation; influences libertarian thought experiments

## 11. Subsistence / Traditional Economy

### Definition

Economic organization based on **direct production for consumption**, **kinship and custom**, and **barter or gift exchange** rather than money, wages, or markets. Common in pre-modern societies and remote communities today.

### Core Rules

1. **Produce what you consume** — minimal surplus trade.
2. **Kinship allocation** — family and clan distribute labor and output.
3. **Customary rights** — land and resources governed by tradition, not deeds.
4. **Reciprocity** — gift and obligation networks replace cash.
5. **Low specialization** — generalist skills across hunting, farming, craft.

### Success Parameters

| Parameter | Target Range | Why It Matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Caloric sufficiency | Population fed year-round | Survival baseline |
| Seasonal resilience | Survive lean seasons via storage or migration | Adaptive capacity |
| Social cohesion | Low internal violence | Reciprocity bonds hold |
| Ecological balance | Sustainable harvest/hunt rates | Long-term viability |

### Failure Signals

- Contact with market economies disrupts reciprocity
- Resource depletion (overhunting, soil exhaustion)
- Forced integration into wage labor (colonialism)
- Climate shocks without surplus buffer

### Examples

Indigenous communal systems, pastoral nomadism, slash-and-burn horticulture societies

## Comparative Summary

| System | Ownership | Allocation | Primary Incentive | Typical Stability Trade-off |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Capitalism** | Private | Markets | Profit | High growth; inequality risk |
| **Socialism** | Social / public | Planned + guided | Social need | Equity; efficiency risk |
| **Tripartism** | Mixed (private base) | Markets + negotiated macro | Consensus / stability | Industrial peace; rigidity risk |
| **Communism** | State | Central plan | Quota fulfillment | Rapid industrialization; shortage risk |
| **Mixed economy** | Mixed | Markets + state | Profit + welfare | Balanced; political conflict |
| **Mercantilism** | State-chartered | Directed trade | National power | Colonial wealth; zero-sum conflict |
| **Feudalism** | Lordly tenure | Custom / obligation | Land & loyalty | Local order; stagnation |
| **Market socialism** | Social (worker/public) | Markets | Worker surplus | Equity + competition; capital constraints |
| **State capitalism** | State | Markets + directive | National development | Fast buildout; corruption risk |
| **Anarcho-capitalism** | Private (total) | Voluntary markets | Profit | Theoretical freedom; public goods gap |
| **Subsistence** | Communal / kin | Custom | Survival | Resilient locally; fragile to shocks |

## Simulation Mapping (Suggested)

When implementing these systems in **The Benevolent Monarch**, each can be parameterized roughly as follows:

```
System {
  ownershipPrivate:     0.0 – 1.0   // share of means of production privately held
  marketAllocation:     0.0 – 1.0   // share of goods priced by supply/demand
  statePlanning:        0.0 – 1.0   // share of output set by central directive
  redistribution:       0.0 – 1.0   // tax + transfer intensity
  tripartiteStrength:   0.0 – 1.0   // labor–capital–state negotiation power
  unionDensity:         0.0 – 1.0   // organized labor coverage
  profitMotivation:     0.0 – 1.0   // firm behavior driven by surplus
  socialProvision:      0.0 – 1.0   // public goods and universal services
}
```

**Composite success score** (example weighting):

| Metric | Weight | Direction |
| --- | --- | --- |
| GDP growth | 15% | Higher is better (to a point) |
| Unemployment | 15% | Lower is better |
| Inflation stability | 10% | Near target is better |
| Gini coefficient | 15% | Lower is better (or system-appropriate) |
| HDI components | 20% | Higher is better |
| Political stability | 10% | Higher is better |
| Productivity growth | 15% | Higher is better |

Adjust weights per system — e.g., socialism weights equity higher; capitalism weights growth and innovation higher; tripartism weights strike rate and wage-productivity alignment higher.
